# Apex Family Legacy

# Artifact Model

## Overview

Artifacts are one of the foundational objects within Apex Family Legacy.

Every scanned document, photograph, audio recording, recipe, letter, certificate, newspaper clipping, or keepsake enters the archive as an **Artifact**.

Artifacts preserve family history.

Some artifacts also serve as evidence.

Understanding this distinction is central to the design of the archive.

---

# Core Principle

An artifact is defined by **what it is**, not by **how it is used**.

For example:

Grandpa's WWII draft notice is always a letter.

It may also serve as official evidence of military service.

Those are two separate concepts.

The artifact's identity never changes.

Its relationships and uses may grow over time.

---

# Artifact Identity

Every artifact should answer the question:

> **What is this?**

Examples include:

* Photograph
* Letter
* Recipe
* Audio Recording
* Video
* Certificate
* Report Card
* Newspaper
* Diary
* Scrapbook Page
* Military Document
* Map
* Book
* Object

The artifact type describes the physical or digital nature of the item.

It does not describe its historical importance.

---

# Artifact Type

Every artifact has exactly one primary type.

Examples:

Photo

Letter

Recipe

Document

Audio Recording

Video

Physical Object

Map

Book

The application should provide a stable set of built-in system types.

Additional user-defined types may be added over time to better organize unique family collections.

Examples:

* School Report Card
* Church Bulletin
* Family Reunion Program
* Quilt Pattern
* Farm Ledger

The archive should grow with the family.

---

# Evidence Classification

Evidence is **not** an artifact type.

Evidence describes the historical authority of an artifact.

Possible system classifications include:

* Official Record
* Primary Source
* Secondary Source
* Personal Artifact
* Family Memory
* Reproduction / Copy
* Unknown

These classifications help genealogists evaluate the strength of evidence while remaining optional for casual users.

An artifact remains an artifact regardless of its evidentiary value.

---

# Relationships Create Meaning

Artifacts gain meaning through connections.

A single photograph connected to nothing is simply an image.

The same photograph connected to:

* People
* Places
* Events
* Stories
* Collections

becomes part of the family's history.

Relationships are what transform an object into a meaningful part of the archive.

---

# Collections

Collections are curated groups of artifacts.

Examples include:

* Christmas Through the Years
* Mom's School Records
* Grandpa's Military Service
* Family Recipes
* LeFort Farm

Collections tell stories.

They are intentionally created by the family.

---

# Tags

Tags provide flexible organization.

Examples:

Christmas

Vacation

Massachusetts

WWII

Elementary School

Farm

Unlike collections, tags are lightweight and primarily assist searching and discovery.

---

# Provenance

Every artifact should preserve its own history.

Examples include:

Original owner

Current owner

Donated by

Scanned by

Digitized on

Physical storage location

Confidence notes

Identification history

Provenance strengthens trust while preserving the history of the artifact itself.

---

# Design Philosophy

The application should never begin by asking:

> "Is this a source?"

Instead it should ask:

> "What is this?"

Once an artifact exists within the archive, it may later support claims, illustrate stories, document events, or simply preserve memories.

Those roles emerge naturally through relationships.

---

# Guiding Principle

**Objects do not belong to categories.**

**Objects accumulate meaning through relationships.**

An artifact's identity remains constant.

Its value grows as additional people, events, places, stories, and memories become connected to it.

This philosophy allows Apex Family Legacy to function as a true digital family archive rather than simply a repository of genealogy records.
